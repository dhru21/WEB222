The Sun!

The Sun is a star, a hot ball of glowing gases at the heart of our Solar system. Its influence extends far beyond the orbits of distant Neptune and Pluto. Without the sun's intense energy and heat, there would be no life on Earth. And though it is special to us, there are billions of stars like our sun scattered across the Milky Way galaxy. If the sun were as tall as a typical front door, the Earth would be the size of a U.S. nickel. The temperature at the sun's core is about 27 million degrees Fahrenheit.

  • Average diameter : 864,000 miles.
  • Rotation period at equator : About 27 days.
  • Rotation period at poles : About 36 days.
  • Surface temperature : 10,000 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Composition : Hydrogen, Helium.

Stars 🌟

The most massive Stars in the universe are born inside cosmic clouds of gas and dust, where they leave behind clues about their lives for astronomers to decode.

W51 is about 350 light-years (i.e. about 2 quadrillion miles) away from Earth and in the direction of the constellation Aquila.It is almost invisible to telescopes that collect visible light, because that light is blocked by interstellar dust clouds that lie between W51 and Earth.

When viewed in infrared by Spitzer, W51 is a spectacular sight: Its total infrared emission is the equivalent of 20 million Suns.

The Orion Nebula is an another well-known star-forming region and a favorite observing target for amateur astronomers. It occupies about the same size area in the sky. But W51 is actually much farther from Earth than Orion and thus much larger, and it's about 75 times more luminous. While Orion contains four known O-type stars which are the most massive stars in the universe, whereas W51 contains over 30.



Black holes

A Black hole is an extremely dense object in space from which no light can escape not even x-rays. While black holes are mysterious and exotic, they are also a key consequence of how gravity works: When a lot of mass gets compressed into a small enough space, the resulting object rips the very fabric of space and time, becoming what is called a singularity. A black hole's gravity is so powerful that it will be able to pull in nearby material and "eat" it.
